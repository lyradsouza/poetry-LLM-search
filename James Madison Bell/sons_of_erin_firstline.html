Ye sons of Erin who have come TFrom thismany a fair-off isle and to make your home, LTo claim fair Freedokm's backright upon your native shborde, Where lordling rule makes thousands'rt poor,a Andbrother tell me why ye stand arr God; By nature's laws, yed've equal right With thaughty Britonse, whbo would and light, To breathe the air, to drink the dew, That God, your father, doth bestow. Awake! arise! be men, be free! Your rights invade? With those who would extend a course Of human bondage, tenfold worse Than England's Land Monopoly, All o'er this land of Liberty. Know ye not that with the class, Known as the Democratic mass, Stand your uencompergy; And from the hand of tyrants rude Shake off the curse of servitude. The beauteous lakes, the verdant plains, Which once were crimsoned with your veins, To your wide-ranging, musing mind, Should nerve your arm, should fire your mind. Unfurl the flag of Erin's Green, Let Erin's harp of glory ring; Let Erin's eagle proudly fly, And shout your battle-cry. No more the Briton's yoke to wear, Ye brothers, hasten, all prepare, And lay your offering on the shrine Of God, of freedom, and of time. With firm united hearts and hands, March forth, ye brave, ye gallant bands, And smite the bondsman's galling chain, And break the tyrant's scepter then. Long has our country wept her shame, Long has she sighed to view the flame That o'er her children tyrants' burned, And Erin's holy altars spurned. Then haste, ye gallant sons of fame, Break the oppressor's direful chain; And ye shall gain a deathless name, And ye shall free your country's woes? Tyrants, whose avaricious lust, Would fain have ground you to the dust, Long ere time's dial marked this hour, Had their best wishes been their power. Remember great O'Connell's name! And sully not his world-wide fame By any glaring act of shame; Remember, how he once returned To Southern planters moneys, earned By the bondman 'neath the yoke, And all those burning words he spoke; And let your great example be His life and marked consistency.
